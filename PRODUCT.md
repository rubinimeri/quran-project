# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Nur serves a broad spectrum of Muslims at once, and the design must hold both
ends without condescending to either:

- **Daily worshippers** — practicing Muslims who return throughout the day to
  read the Qur'an, check prayer times, count dhikr, and recite supplications.
  Often on mobile, frequently in quiet, reflective moments (after salah, before
  sleep, in the morning). They value calm, speed, and reliability.
- **Learners & new Muslims** — people studying the text who lean on
  transliteration and translation alongside the Arabic. They need the meaning to
  feel approachable and the interface to feel welcoming, never intimidating.

The shared context is reverent and personal, not productive. People come to Nur
to connect with the words of Allah, not to "get through tasks."

## Product Purpose

Nur ("light") is a modern home for the Qur'an and daily Islamic practice. It
brings reading (Surahs and verses with translation/transliteration), prayer
times (Salah), supplications (Du'ā), and a dhikr counter (Tasbeeh) into one
coherent, beautiful experience.

Success looks like an app people *want* to open — one that makes scripture and
worship feel inviting and dignified, where the aesthetic itself is part of the
draw. Because this is a brand-register product, the feel and identity lead: Nur
should be the Qur'an app someone keeps because it is beautiful, calm, and warm,
not merely functional.

## Brand Personality

**Warm · Devotional · Approachable.**

Sincere and heartfelt without being austere; reverent without being cold or
formal. The voice is gentle and welcoming — it honors the sacredness of the text
while making it feel within reach for everyone, from the lifelong reciter to the
person opening a Qur'an for the first time. Light (literal and spiritual) is the
guiding metaphor: the experience should feel luminous, serene, and quietly
generous.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS dashboards.** No cold corporate tool aesthetic — no stat-card
  grids, blue gradients, KPI tiles, or charty "analytics" framing. This is
  worship, not metrics. Counters and prayer times are devotional instruments,
  not data widgets.
- **Austere / intimidating religiosity.** Not severe, gatekeeping, or overly
  formal. Nothing that makes a newcomer feel they don't belong.
- (Also avoid, by extension) the cluttered, ad-heavy, feature-crammed UI of
  legacy Quran/prayer apps; and any childish/cartoonish treatment that would
  undercut the reverence.

## Design Principles

1. **The words come first.** The interface is a frame for scripture and dhikr,
   never the subject. When in doubt, remove chrome so the Arabic and its meaning
   take center stage.
2. **Calm is a feature.** Generous whitespace, unhurried motion, and restraint
   are functional — they create the reflective space worship needs. Never busy,
   never loud.
3. **Warmth without compromise on reverence.** Be inviting and human, but always
   dignified. Beauty serves devotion; it is not decoration for its own sake.
4. **Welcome every level.** Transliteration, translation, and clear navigation
   make the sacred approachable. Default to inclusion over assumed expertise.
5. **Light as identity.** Lean into "Nur" — luminous accents (gold), gentle
   glows, and a sense of illumination carry the brand, not heavy ornamentation.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **Target WCAG 2.1 AA**: ≥4.5:1 contrast for body text, ≥3:1 for large text,
  full keyboard navigation, visible and strong focus states.
- **Extra reading support** (this app is read for long, devotional stretches):
  - Generous line-height for Arabic script; never let diacritics crowd.
  - Comfortable measure and scalable text for translation/transliteration.
  - Clear, persistent focus indicators for long sessions.
- **Honor `prefers-reduced-motion`**: every animation needs a reduced-motion
  fallback (crossfade or instant). Motion is enhancement, never a gate on
  content visibility.
